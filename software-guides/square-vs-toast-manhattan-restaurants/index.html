Square vs Toast for Manhattan restaurants and bars.

For most Manhattan bars, restaurants, and cafes, Toast is stronger when the operation needs restaurant-native ordering, kitchen routing, tabs, modifiers, tips, and service reporting. Square is usually a better fit when the business is smaller, simpler, retail-adjacent, or needs lighter payments and lower setup complexity.

Local context

Why New York changes the decision.

Manhattan changes the decision because rent is high, counter space is tight, staff turnover is real, and payment friction shows up immediately during rushes. The right POS is not the one with the longest feature list. It is the one your staff can use under pressure.

Workflow first. Software second.

The best tool is the one that supports how customers find you, how staff capture the action, and how the work moves after that.

Keep / Connect / Replace / Build

Do not buy software before you check the fit.

Keep

What works

Keep the POS that staff already use well. Keep payment hardware if it is reliable and supported.

Connect

What matters

Connect menu pages, ordering links, Google profile, and analytics. Connect lead capture for private events or catering.

Replace

What drags

Replace duplicate ordering links and stale menu embeds. Replace tools that fail during service or make reporting manual.

Build

What fits

Build a cleaner website-to-ordering path. Build an owner dashboard for sales signals, reviews, and open loops.

FAQ

Fast answers.

Is Toast always better for restaurants?

No. Toast is restaurant-native, but some small cafes and counter-service shops are better served by Square.

Can Square work for a Manhattan bar?

Sometimes, especially for simple payment flows. Bars with tabs, modifiers, and high-volume service usually need deeper hospitality features.

Should the POS decide the website?

No. The customer path should decide what the website needs to connect to.
