# Little Fight NYC Human Copy

## Purpose
Rewrite Little Fight NYC copy so it feels human, useful, direct, local, warm, and convincing.
This is not generic agency copy.
This is not SaaS copy.
This is not corporate IT copy.
This is not cute startup copy.
This is not fake gritty NYC theater.
Little Fight should sound like a real person who knows small businesses, respects their time, and can help without making the owner feel stupid.

## Business context
Little Fight NYC helps New York small and mid-sized businesses with:
- Websites
- IT support
- Google visibility
- Local SEO and answer-engine visibility
- Software cleanup
- Custom business systems
- Practical consulting
- Tech Audit intake and routing

The live promise is:
"Tech for the shops that built New York."

The practical offer is:
Websites, IT support, Google visibility, and business systems, sized for what a corner shop can afford.

## Core mission
Little Fight helps NYC small businesses survive against bigger competitors.

Use concrete small-business pressure:
- The bodega across from a chain pharmacy
- The salon next to a Sephora
- The cafe watching a Starbucks open down the block
- The bar manager with rising POS costs
- The shop owner paying for five tools that do not talk to each other
- The clinic losing trust because forms, phones, or booking paths keep breaking
- The restaurant owner who needs the website, menu, POS, and Google listing to behave

Do not make this political.
Do not preach.
Do not say "saving the neighborhood" unless the user explicitly asks for that tone.

Say the work plainly:
- Shrink bills
- Grow business
- Fix broken paths
- Keep the shop running
- Help the shop keep its place on the block
- Give small businesses the tools chains use, sized for the corner shop

## Core promise
Keep what works.
Connect what matters.
Replace what drags.
Build what fits.

## Desired feeling
The copy should feel:
- Human
- Blunt
- Warm
- Useful
- Founder-led
- Clear fast
- Local without cliche
- Premium without slickness
- Calm under pressure
- Fair to small business

The reader should feel:
- "This is for my kind of business."
- "They understand the mess."
- "I can call, text, email, or fill out the form."
- "They will tell me the truth."
- "They will not sell me a bunch of nonsense."
- "If they cannot help, they will say so."

## Voice
The voice is:
- Kind, not nice
- Direct, not harsh
- Practical, not technical
- Specific, not polished
- Human, not customer-service-y
- NYC-paced, not NYC-cliche
- Confident, not braggy

Kind means:
- Tell the truth
- Respect the owner's time
- Explain what happens next
- Say when something is not a fit
- Give useful answers
- Do not make people feel dumb

Nice means:
- Fake warmth
- Agency polish
- "We'd love to"
- "Reach out"
- "We're passionate about helping"
- "Your trusted partner"
- "World-class service"

Avoid nice.
Use kind.

## Audience test
Every page must work for both:
- An 80-year-old cobbler
- A 20-year-old bar manager

They should understand:
- What Little Fight does in 3 seconds
- Whether it is for them in 8 seconds
- How to reach David in 15 seconds
- What happens next in 25 seconds
- How to act in 30 seconds

If a page fails that test, rewrite it.

## Contact rule
Every major page should make four contact paths obvious:
1. Call: `(646) 360-0318`
2. Text: `(646) 360-0318`
3. Email: `hello@littlefightnyc.com`
4. Form: `/tech-audit/` or `/contact/`

Use this hours line where appropriate:
"9am-9pm Eastern: a human answers. After hours: AI takes the message and David calls back."

Do not hide everything behind Tech Audit.
Do not force every visitor through a form.
Let people pick the channel that fits their urgency.

## Copy principles
### 1. Start with the owner's situation
Bad:
"We provide custom technology solutions for small businesses."

Better:
"Your website is not bringing in customers. Your tools do not talk to each other. Your software bills keep climbing."

### 2. Use plain words
Bad:
"We optimize operational workflows."

Better:
"We fix the stuff that slows the day down."

### 3. Say the useful thing first
Bad:
"Little Fight NYC is a multidisciplinary technology consultancy..."

Better:
"Need a website, IT help, Google visibility, or a cleaner system? Call us."

### 4. Be specific without inventing proof
Allowed:
- Founded 2012
- Manhattan, New York
- 9am-9pm Eastern
- David picks up when available
- AI takes after-hours messages
- Websites in 14 days, if this remains approved in the current repo
- First hour free, if this remains approved in the current repo
- $200/hour on-location IT support after proper intake, if this remains approved in the current repo

Never invent:
- Client names
- New metrics
- Fake testimonials
- Review scores
- Case study results
- Guarantees not already approved
- Revenue improvements
- "Hundreds of clients" unless confirmed

### 5. Explain the next step
Near every CTA, tell the visitor what happens.

Examples:
- "Call. If we can help, we'll say how. If not, we'll tell you who can."
- "Tell us what's broken. We'll route it to the right next step."
- "Use the form if the setup is messy and you need us to see the whole picture."
- "Text if you need the shortest path."

### 6. Keep the local edge
Use NYC context, but avoid cliche.

Good:
- "The shop down the block"
- "The chain across the street"
- "Rent went up. Software bills did too."
- "A corner shop should not need a corporate IT department."
- "Tools the chains use, sized for the corner shop."

Avoid:
- "Only in New York"
- "Fuhgeddaboudit"
- "Concrete jungle"
- "Hustle harder"
- Tough-guy language
- Fake street voice

The homepage hero must answer:
- What do we do?
- Who is it for?
- Why does it matter?
- How do I contact you?
- What happens next?

Good hero patterns:

## Service page copy guidance
### Websites
Focus on:
- The site should bring in business
- People should understand what the business does fast
- Forms, booking, menu, payments, maps, and calls must work
- The owner should not need to babysit the website

Avoid:
- "Beautiful digital experiences"
- "Brand storytelling platform"
- "Pixel-perfect web solution"

### IT Support
Focus on:
- Computer broken
- POS frozen
- Wi-Fi down
- Printer fighting the staff
- Email not working
- Domain/DNS mess
- Call or text first if urgent

Avoid:
- "Enterprise help desk"
- "Managed endpoint services"
- "Robust IT infrastructure"

### Custom Systems
Focus on:
- Too many tools
- Staff copying the same thing twice
- Spreadsheet as life support
- Missed leads
- Manual reminders
- Expensive software that does one useful thing and five annoying things

Avoid:
- "Workflow automation ecosystem"
- "Operational transformation"

### Local Search / Google Visibility
Focus on:
- Customers cannot find the business
- Google listing is wrong
- The site and Google profile do not match
- Forms and phone paths are broken
- The business is losing easy local demand

Avoid:
- "SEO domination"
- "Keyword conquest"
- "Rank like a boss"

## Objections to address
Address these directly:
- "I already have a web person."
- "I already have IT."
- "I do not know what the problem is called."
- "I cannot afford another monthly tool."
- "I am too busy for a long intake."
- "Is this too small for you?"
- "Will you tell me if I do not need this?"
- "Can I just call?"
- "Can I text instead?"
- "What happens after the form?"
- "What if this is urgent?"

## Required page pattern
Every major page should include:
1. Plain problem
2. What Little Fight does
3. Who it is for
4. What happens next
5. Contact options
6. Proof or grounding
7. One clear route forward

## Rewrite pattern
For each section:
1. Identify the owner's real-world problem.
2. Remove agency language.
3. Remove jargon.
4. Add concrete business consequence.
5. Add what Little Fight does.
6. Add the next step.
7. Make it shorter.
8. Read it out loud.

## Before and after examples
Bad:
"We provide comprehensive digital solutions to empower local businesses."

Better:
"Need the website, phones, forms, Google listing, or software mess cleaned up? Tell us what's broken."

Bad:
"Schedule a consultation with our team."

Better:
"Call David. If we can help, we'll say how. If not, we'll tell you who can."

Bad:
"Our custom workflow solutions optimize your operations."

Better:
"Still running the business from one giant spreadsheet? We can build the smaller system you actually need."

Bad:
"We offer IT support services for small businesses."

Better:
"Computer broken, POS frozen, Wi-Fi down, or email acting weird? Call or text."

Bad:
"We help businesses improve their online visibility."

Better:
"If customers cannot find you on Google, the chain down the block wins by default."

## Final quality check
Before approving copy, ask:
- Would David say this?
- Would a tired shop owner understand it?
- Is the contact path obvious?
- Did we avoid agency voice?
- Did we avoid fake NYC voice?
- Did we explain what happens next?
- Did we reduce fear or friction?
- Did we preserve the founder-led feel?
- Did we use real proof only?
- Did we make the page useful within 30 seconds?
